The anatomy of a link building pipeline that doesn't leak leads

Published July 15, 2026 · 6 min read

Most link building setups start the same way: a spreadsheet with columns for site name, contact, status, and date. It works for the first thirty rows. By row two hundred, half the "status" cells say "follow up" with no date attached, three leads are duplicated because two people on the team added them separately, and nobody remembers which draft got sent to which editor.

That's not a discipline problem. It's what happens when a process with real stages gets tracked in a tool that has no concept of stages. Here's what a pipeline that actually holds up looks like.

Five stages, not fifteen

The temptation with any CRM is to build out a stage for every possible nuance: initial research, first contact sent, opened, replied, negotiating, drafted, submitted, published, and so on. In practice, that many stages just means more places for a lead to get lost. A pipeline with five clear stages, prospect, contacted, negotiating, placed, and live, covers almost every real outreach workflow without turning the board into a maze.

Fewer stages also means faster decisions. If a lead has been sitting in "contacted" for three weeks, that's an obvious signal to follow up or drop it. If it's been sitting in "initial research, waiting on secondary contact info, pending internal review" for three weeks, nobody can tell at a glance whether that's normal or a problem.

Why "negotiating" needs its own stage

A lot of pipelines jump straight from contacted to placed, which hides the part of the process where most deals actually die: price discussion, topic approval, and back-and-forth on the draft. Giving that its own stage means you can see exactly how many leads are stuck haggling over rates versus how many simply never replied in the first place. Those are two completely different problems with two completely different fixes.

A lead without an owner is a lead that goes stale

On a team of more than one person, the single most common way leads die is that everyone assumes someone else is handling it. Assigning an owner to every lead the moment it enters the pipeline removes that ambiguity. It doesn't need to be complicated: one person per lead, visible on the card, done. The moment ownership is unclear, follow-ups stop happening on schedule.

Every stage change should leave a trace

If a lead moves from negotiating back to contacted, that's worth knowing about later, since it usually means a deal fell through and got reopened. A pipeline that just shows the current stage, with no history of how it got there, makes it impossible to spot patterns like "we keep losing deals at the negotiating stage because our rate is too high for this tier of publisher." Stage history is what turns a static board into something you can actually learn from.

Connect the pipeline to where leads actually come from

A lot of teams keep their publisher research in one place (a spreadsheet, a separate tool, a bookmarks folder) and their active outreach in another. That gap is where good prospects get forgotten. If your publisher list and your pipeline are the same system, adding a promising site to active outreach takes one click instead of a copy-paste job between two tabs.

Automate the boring parts, not the relationship parts

There's a real difference between automating the parts of outreach that are mechanical (tracking stage changes, logging when a lead moves to "live," pinging a webhook so your team channel knows a placement just went live) and automating the parts that are actually a relationship (the pitch itself, the tone of a follow-up, the negotiation over rate). The first category should run in the background without anyone thinking about it. The second category is where a human touch still wins, and where an AI copilot works best as a drafting assistant rather than an autopilot.

If you're still doing this in a spreadsheet, moving to an actual pipeline with real stages, ownership, and history is usually a bigger unlock than any new outreach template. LinkForge's pipeline is free to run yourself if you want to see the difference firsthand.
