What publishers actually look for in a guest post pitch

Published July 21, 2026 · 6 min read

We asked a handful of editors and site owners who regularly accept guest posts what actually separates a pitch they say yes to from the hundred they don't. None of them mentioned domain authority. Almost all of them mentioned some version of the same thing: does this person clearly understand what my site is for.

That's a lower bar than most people think, and a higher one than most pitches actually clear. Here's what came up again and again.

They can tell within one paragraph if you've actually read the site

Every editor we spoke to said some version of "I can tell in the first two sentences whether this is a form letter." The tell isn't complicated: generic compliments, no mention of a specific post, a topic that could have been pitched to any site in the niche. One editor described it as "the email equivalent of a form letter that starts with Dear Valued Customer."

What actually reads as genuine is a specific reference: a post you disagreed with slightly, a gap in their coverage, a comment thread where readers were clearly asking for something the site hasn't covered yet. That level of detail takes real time to gather, which is exactly why it works. It's proof of effort that can't be faked in bulk.

They want to know what happens after they say yes

A surprising number of editors said their biggest frustration isn't bad pitches, it's writers who disappear or go quiet after getting a yes. A pitch that briefly mentions your turnaround time and what the editing process looks like on your end (do you take feedback well, do you hit deadlines) sets expectations before there's a problem to manage. It's a small detail, but it signals you've done this before and know what a working relationship actually involves.

Writing samples matter less than editors let on

Several editors admitted they barely look at attached writing samples unless the pitch itself is promising. The pitch email is the actual audition. If it's well organized, specific, and free of obvious errors, that tells them more about the eventual draft than three linked articles will.

They notice when the ask matches the site's actual audience

A pitch about advanced technical SEO sent to a site that mostly covers small business marketing basics is a mismatch that's obvious to the person reading it, even if the topic itself is good. Understanding who actually reads the site, not just what the site covers, is the difference between a pitch that gets a "this isn't really for us" and one that gets a "yes, and here's an angle we'd love even more."

They remember who followed up well and who didn't

More than one editor mentioned keeping a mental list of writers who handled a rejection gracefully versus ones who got pushy or argumentative. Guest posting is a small world within any given niche, and a professional response to a no (even a simple "totally understand, keep me in mind for future topics") is what gets someone invited back six months later when the site's needs change.

The pattern underneath all of it

Every point above comes down to the same thing: treating the pitch as a conversation with one specific person rather than a template aimed at a category of website. That's slower per pitch. It's also the entire reason a small number of well-researched pitches consistently outperforms a large number of generic ones, even when the generic batch goes out to objectively better sites.

If you're managing outreach at any real scale, keeping notes on what worked with which publisher matters as much as the initial pitch.
